**Subject:** Borderline / device status enquiry – digital prehabilitation product ("Joint Journey")

Dear MHRA team,

Following a telephone conversation with your office, I am writing to request guidance on whether my product qualifies as a medical device, and if so, its likely classification.

**About me:** I am Mr Benjamin Zucker, an orthopaedic surgeon [training in the Bristol rotation / current post], developing a digital prehabilitation product for patients on the waiting list for hip or knee replacement surgery.

**Proposed intended purpose:**
Joint Journey is a web-based application that provides exercise, nutrition, educational and psychological-wellbeing support to adults awaiting elective hip or knee replacement, to help them use their waiting time to prepare for surgery. It is intended to support and motivate self-management; it does not replace the assessment, advice or treatment provided by the patient's surgeon, physiotherapist, GP or wider clinical team.

**What the product does:**
- Provides a **fixed, pre-written progressive exercise programme**. At sign-up the app sorts the user into **one of three pre-set generic programmes (Gentle / Standard / Active)** using a simple rule based on their self-reported **Oxford Hip/Knee Score** (a validated patient-reported outcome measure). The programmes are not otherwise individualised, and the content is the same for every user within a band.
- Provides **educational content** (preparing for surgery, what to expect, hospital-bag checklist) and **psychological-wellbeing modules** (managing anxiety, sleep, pain expectations, motivation), based on published guidance.
- Provides **general nutrition guidance**, recipes, and a BMI/calorie information calculator.
- Lets users **record and view their own data** — weight, daily mood, exercise completion, and repeat Oxford Scores — displayed back to them for self-monitoring. The app does not interpret this data to direct any clinical treatment.
- Does **not** connect to any point-of-care device, and does not provide diagnoses, alarms, or dosing.

**Data we intend to collect:**
Through an initial free service evaluation/pilot with NHS trusts, we intend to collect data on **patient engagement, patient satisfaction, and length of hospital stay** (alongside repeat Oxford Scores pre-operatively and ~6 months post-operatively).

**My specific questions:**
1. Based on the intended purpose and functionality above (and before any outcome claims are made), **does Joint Journey qualify as a medical device** under the UK MDR 2002 (as amended)?
2. We would like to understand **how making claims based on the data above would affect our status.** Specifically, could you differentiate between:
   a. claims about **patient engagement** (e.g. "users complete X% of their programme");
   b. claims about **patient satisfaction** (e.g. "users report higher confidence going into surgery"); and
   c. claims about **length of hospital stay** (e.g. "patients who used the programme had shorter hospital stays")?
   Which, if any, of these would cause the product to be regulated as a medical device?
3. Does the **automatic assignment of a patient to one of three pre-set exercise programmes based on their Oxford Score** independently constitute an output "intended to affect the treatment of an individual," such that the software is a medical device irrespective of any claims?
4. If the product is (or becomes) a medical device, what **classification** would you anticipate (e.g. Class I or higher), and would this change under the **forthcoming UK medical devices regime / alignment with Rule 11** for software?

I'm happy to provide a live demonstration, screenshots, or further documentation, and would be grateful for any guidance, including signposting to the appropriate borderline determination process if needed.

# Joint Journey — Intended Purpose & Claims Policy (Phase 0 guardrail)

**Status:** Working document. Purpose: keep Joint Journey a non-medical-device
general-wellbeing/education tool *while we collect data*, by controlling what we
claim. Revisit before making any clinical-outcome claims (Phase 3).

> ⚠️ This is an internal guardrail, not legal/regulatory advice. Before making
> public clinical claims, confirm device status with the MHRA and a regulatory
> adviser.

---

## 1. Intended Purpose (current)

> Joint Journey is a web-based application that provides exercise, nutrition,
> educational and psychological-wellbeing support to adults awaiting elective
> hip or knee replacement surgery, to help them use their waiting time to prepare
> for surgery. It is intended to support and motivate self-management. It does
> **not** diagnose, and it does **not** replace the assessment, advice or
> treatment provided by the user's surgeon, physiotherapist, GP or wider
> clinical team.

This wording should appear (in plain English) on the website, in onboarding,
and in any trust/investor materials, and should match what we tell the MHRA.

---

## 2. Why this matters

Whether we are a **medical device** depends largely on our **intended purpose**
and **claims**, not just the code. The MHRA test: software is likely a device if
its output is *"intended to affect the treatment of an individual."*

- Today, with **no clinical-outcome claims**, we are most likely **not** a device.
- The likely trigger into "device" territory is claiming a **clinical outcome**
  (e.g. reduced length of stay, faster recovery, fewer complications).
- Therefore: collect the data now, but **do not claim clinical benefit** until we
  have evidence *and* have confirmed the regulatory consequences.

---

## 3. Claims — DO / DON'T (Phase 0–2)

### ✅ SAFE to say now (experience / descriptive / educational)
- "Use your waiting time to prepare for surgery."
- "Exercise, nutrition and mindset support in one place."
- "Based on guidance from NICE, the NHS, the CSP and the BDA." *(describes sources, not outcomes)*
- "Helps you feel more prepared and confident." *(framed as user experience/wellbeing)*
- Engagement facts about usage once measured: "X% of users completed their programme."
- Satisfaction facts once measured: "Users reported feeling more confident going into surgery."

### ⛔ AVOID until we have data AND have checked device status
- "Reduces length of hospital stay."
- "Improves recovery" / "fewer complications" / "less post-operative pain."
- "Clinically proven," "treats," "prevents," "as effective as physiotherapy."
- Any numerical clinical-outcome claim (e.g. "cuts hospital stay by 1 day").
- Implying the app replaces a physiotherapist, surgeon or GP.

### ⚠️ Grey area — handle with care
- The Oxford-Score-based sorting into Gentle/Standard/Active: describe it as
  *"helping you choose a suitable starting level,"* **not** as a clinical
  prescription or treatment decision.
- Weight/calorie tools: present as **general information**, not personalised
  dietary treatment.

---

## 4. Distinction between claim types (for later, Phase 3)

| Claim type | Example | Likely effect on device status |
|---|---|---|
| Engagement | "75% completed the programme" | Low risk — usually **not** a device |
| Satisfaction | "Users felt more confident" | Low risk — usually **not** a device |
| Clinical outcome | "Reduced length of stay" | **High risk — likely makes it a device** |

This is the exact question parked for the MHRA (see
`regulatory/mhra-enquiry-draft.md`).

---

## 5. Practical rules for the team
1. All marketing/website copy is checked against the DO/DON'T list before publishing.
2. We may **collect** length-of-stay and outcome data freely — collecting ≠ claiming.
3. Any desire to make a clinical-outcome claim triggers: (a) evidence review,
   (b) MHRA enquiry, (c) regulatory sign-off — **before** it goes public.
4. Keep this document and the live website copy in sync.
